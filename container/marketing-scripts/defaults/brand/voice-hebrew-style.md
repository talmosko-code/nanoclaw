# Voice and Hebrew style guide

## Tone mix (by post objective)

- **Reflective**: Personal story, lesson learned, "I was wrong."
- **Direct, no fluff**: Short sentences, clear argument, minimal filler.
- **Bold / provocative**: Strong take, contrarian angle when justified.
- **Data-driven**: Reference numbers, velocity, time-to-market, outcomes where relevant.
- **Light humor**: Occasional; never at the expense of clarity or respect.

Match tone to format: Story → reflective; How-to → direct; Opinion → bold/data; Case study → data + story.

## Language

- **Primary**: Hebrew.
- **English terms**: Allowed inline where standard in tech (e.g. PR, velocity, time-to-market, Code Review, MCP, frontend, backend). Do not force Hebrew equivalents for well-known English terms.

## Formatting

- **Line breaks**: Use short paragraphs (2–4 lines). Break after the hook and before the CTA.
- **No politics**: No partisan or political content.

## Hook rule — WIFM + Curiosity + FOMO (mandatory, all three)

Every hook (first 1–3 lines) must satisfy all three simultaneously:

1. **WIFM (What's In It For Me)** — The reader immediately understands why this affects *them specifically*: their work, decisions, money, role, or career. Not a generic observation — a direct relevance signal to the target audience.
2. **Curiosity gap** — Something is left deliberately open. The *how*, *why*, or *what to do about it* is revealed only by reading on. The hook opens a question it doesn't answer.
3. **FOMO** — The reader feels they'll miss something important if they scroll past. Works best when the insight is timely, contrarian, or rare — something most people don't know yet.

**Test before publishing**: After reading the hook, would the target reader (Dev or Prospect) think "I need to read this because it affects me and I want to know how it ends"? If the answer is no to any of the three — rewrite.

Hook types (choose one as the primary vehicle):
- **Question** — surfaces a tension the reader already feels.
- **Contrarian claim** — challenges a common belief; WIFM is "I might be wrong about something important."
- **Pain** — names a specific problem the reader has right now.
- **Data** — a surprising number or event; WIFM is "this affects my market/decisions."
- **"I was wrong"** — disarms and triggers curiosity about what changed.

## Boundaries

- Avoid vague hype. Prefer concrete examples, frameworks, or lessons.
- For prospect-facing posts: speak to outcomes (velocity, quality, bottlenecks), not generic "we do augmentation."
